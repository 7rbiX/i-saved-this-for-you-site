ok come sit. turn the big light off, the small one's fine. i went looking for the oldest love poem i could find and i ended up researching just sitting here at 2am with a stranger from 1200 BC who said the sound of someone's voice was pomegranate wine to him. and i thought — nothing has changed. nothing. we are still doing this exact thing.

so here's a little stack of people, three thousand years deep, all saying the same thing in different costumes. sappho. an anonymous egyptian. milton, who was sad. a town poet from dundee who really meant it. read them slow. they're not going anywhere — they already waited this long.

(the oldest one is at the bottom. i couldn't put it first, it felt like spoiling the ending.)

↓ the second-oldest heartbreak on file

"Like the very gods in my sight is he who sits where he can look in your eyes, who listens close to you, to hear the soft voice, its sweetness murmur in love and laughter, all for him. But it breaks my spirit; underneath my breast all the heart is shaken."

— Sappho, translated by Mary Barnard bopsecrets.org · Archive of Sappho translations

sappho's fragment 31 — the oldest jealousy on record, and still no cure for it. she watches someone she loves sit and laugh with someone else and her whole body just... goes. 600 BC. nothing has changed.

"To hear your voice is pomegranate wine to me: I draw life from hearing it."

— Ancient Egyptian Love Poem (New Kingdom, c. 1200 BC) National Geographic · Ancient Egyptian Poetry

three thousand years old and it still lands. that's not trying to be clever. that's just real desire saying itself. this is the one. this is the one that made me build the whole page.

Open my heart and you will see
Graved inside of it, "Italy."
Such lovers old are I and she:
So it always was, so shall ever be!
— Robert Browning, De Gustibus— · PoetryDB

a place becoming the whole landscape of your soul. forever isn't flowers here — it's just italy carved into the chest. (browning loved her so much he loved her country.)

i keep thinking about the gap between sappho and that egyptian poem. 600 years. and they're saying the same thing. →

found · sappho, fragment 16

Some say a cavalry corps,
some infantry, some, again,
will maintain that the swift oars
of our fleet are the finest
sight on dark earth; but I say
that whatever one loves, is.

— Mary Barnard, Sappho: A New Translation, 1958 · Wikipedia

"that whatever one loves, is." seven syllables and it's a whole philosophy. she's saying forget the armies and the ships — the most beautiful thing on earth is whatever YOUR heart is pointed at. i had to put my phone down after this one.

poem · the brave & honest one

We lov'd, and we lov'd, as long as we could, Till our love was lov'd out in us both: But our marriage is dead, when the pleasure is fled: 'Twas pleasure first made it an oath.

— John Dryden · Why Should A Foolish Marriage Vow · PoetryDB

and then dryden, just being brutally honest — love burns out and that's not a tragedy, it's just weather. i don't fully agree with him?? but i respect that he said it out loud while everyone else was writing roses.

poem · pure yearning

Come live with me, and be my love, And we will all the pleasures prove, That hills and valleys, dales and fields, And all the craggy mountain yields. There we will sit upon the rocks, And see the shepherds feed their flocks By shallow rivers, to whose falls Melodious birds sing madrigals.

— Christopher Marlowe · The Passionate Shepherd to His Love · PoetryDB

marlowe building an entire world out of wanting someone. come live with me, i'll give you mountains, i'll give you birds, i'll give you everything — it's desperate and it's gorgeous and we all do exactly this when we fall.

At A Solemn Musick
Dead things with inbreath'd sense able to pierce,
And to our high-rais'd phantasie present,
That undisturbed Song of pure content,

As once we did, till disproportion'd sin
Jarr'd against natures chime, and with harsh din
The fair musick that all creatures made
— John Milton  ·  PoetryDB

milton holds up this memory of everything in creation singing in tune — and then one word, Jarr'd, breaks it. that's the sound of paradise going out of key. the whole poem is just him aching to hear the original music again. aren't we all.

1819 New Year's Carrier's Address
Hail home! sacred home! to my soul ever dear;
Abroad may be wonders but rapture is here.
My future ambition will never soar higher
Than the clean brushed hearth and convivial fire;
— Major Henry Livingston, Jr.  ·  PoetryDB · public domain
a love letter to a room. he's saying: keep your wonders, give me the warm fire and the people next to it. somebody who knows exactly where the magic lives and isn't embarrassed about it.
The Late Sir John Ogilvy
"
Because such men as Sir John Ogilvy are hard to be found,
Especially in Christian charity his large heart did abound,
Therefore a monument should be erected for him most handsome to behold,
And his good deeds engraven thereon in letters of gold.
— William Topaz McGonagall  ·  PoetryDB
ok and this one — mcgonagall is famously the "worst poet" but i won't hear it. this awkward, stubborn belief that goodness deserves to be carved in GOLD and remembered forever. love isn't always pretty meter. sometimes it's a working man clumsily insisting that kindness matters.

poem

O Beauty, Passing Beauty!

The bare word “kiss” hath made my inner soul To tremble like a lute string, ere the note Hath melted in the silence that it broke.
— Lord Alfred Tennyson, PoetryDB

just the WORD "kiss" makes him tremble like a plucked string. not the kiss — the word. the anticipation more powerful than the thing. tennyson knew.

poem · the one this page is named after

Amoretti LXXIV: Most Happy Letters

Most happy letters, fram’d by skilful trade, With which that happy name was first design’d: The which three times thrice happy hath me made, With gifts of body, fortune, and of mind. […] To speak her praise and glory excellent, Of all alive most worthy to be praised.
— Edmund Spenser, PoetryDB

he keeps saying "happy." over and over, he can't stop — one time isn't enough to hold it. "most happy LETTERS" — he's literally thanking the alphabet for spelling her name. this is where the page got its title.
