On His Deceased Wife

METHOUGHT I saw my late espoused Saint
   Brought to me like Alcestis from the grave,
   Whom Joves great Son to her glad Husband gave,
   Rescu'd from death by force though pale and faint.
Mine as whom washt from spot of child-bed taint,
   Purification in the old Law did save,
   And such, as yet once more I trust to have
   Full sight of her in Heaven without restraint;
Came vested all in white, pure as her mind:
   Her face was vail'd, yet to my fancied sight,
   Love, sweetness, goodness, in her person shin'd
So clear, as in no face with more delight.
   But O as to embrace me she enclin'd
   I wak'd, she fled, and day brought back my night.
— John Milton, PoetryDB

"i wak'd, she fled, and day brought back my night." he dreams he's about to hold her and then he wakes up and morning is the dark part. milton was blind — he never even saw her face. i had to stop reading on the bus. this is the one that started the pile.

To My Brother George

Many the wonders I this day have seen:
The sun, when first he kissed away the tears
That filled the eyes of Morn;—the laurelled peers
Who from the feathery gold of evening lean;—
The ocean with its vastness, its blue green,
Its ships, its rocks, its caves, its hopes, its fears,
Its voice mysterious, which whoso hears
Must think on what will be, and what has been.
— John Keats, PoetryDB

keats saw all these wonders — the sun kissing away the tears of morning, the whole mysterious ocean — and the thing he actually wanted was to TELL his brother. the reason beauty exists is so you can turn to someone and say did you see that.

To a Gentleman and Lady on the Death of the Lady's Brother and Sister

Methinks I hear her in the realms above,
And leaning forward with a filial love,
Invite you there to share immortal bliss
Unknown, untasted in a state like this.
— Phillis Wheatley, PoetryDB

wheatley doesn't make death a dark tyrant — she imagines the dead leaning toward the living, inviting them on. consolation that doesn't look away from the grief, it just holds both at once. i don't know how she did that.

The inundation of the Spring

The inundation of the Spring
Enlarges every soul --
It sweeps the tenement away
But leaves the Water whole --

In which the soul at first estranged --
Seeks faintly for its shore
But acclimated -- pines no more
For that Peninsula --
— Emily Dickinson, PoetryDB

grief takes the solid ground but it doesn't break you — you just become someone who lives in the water now. the soul gets "acclimated" and stops pining. not because it forgot. because it learned to float. i think about this line a lot.

Time of Roses

It was the time of roses—
We pluck'd them as we pass'd!

That churlish season never frown'd
On early lovers yet:
O no—the world was newly crown'd
With flowers when first we met!
— Thomas Hood, PoetryDB

"we pluck'd them as we pass'd!" the breathless joy of new love that already knows it's fleeting. the repeating "time of roses" is them trying to hold the moment with both hands even while it goes.

To the Memory of Henry Welles Livingston

Adieu! Adieu! My sweest boy,
Adieu till life's vain dream be o'er;
Then with a parent's keenest joy
I'll cling to Thee to part no more.
— Major Henry Livingston, Jr., PoetryDB

a father writing to his dead child. "keenest" means sharpest AND most intense — and a parent's grief is exactly both at once. note the typo "sweest" — i left it, it's in the source, and somehow it makes it worse. realer.

poem — PoetryDB
Oh! Think Not My Spirits Are Always As Light
And the heart that is soonest awake to the flowers,
Is always the first to be touch’d by the thorns.
— Thomas Moore  ·  PoetryDB
the open heart, the one alive to every flower, is the same one that gets caught by every thorn. it's not a warning. it's moore gently saying: yes. i know you feel everything. that's both the gift and the wound.

poem — PoetryDB
Stanzas
Ah! would ‘twere so with many A gentle girl and boy! But were there ever any Writhed not at passed joy? To know the change and feel it, When there is none to heal it, Nor numbed sense to steal it, Was never said in rhyme.
— John Keats  ·  PoetryDB
keats names the one thing nature never has to suffer — the memory of joy. trees forget their green, brooks forget summer. but we remember, and the remembering is what wrings us out. he's almost angry he was the first to say it in rhyme.

poem

Easter Day

My heart stole back across wide wastes of years To One who wandered by a lonely sea, And sought in vain for any place of rest: 'Foxes have holes, and every bird its nest. I, only I, must wander wearily, And bruise my feet, and drink wine salt with tears.'
— Oscar Wilde, PoetryDB

wilde, of all people, drops all the gold crowns and pageantry and remembers someone who had nothing — not even a place to lay his head. "wine salt with tears." it's so quiet about its loneliness that it lands harder.

poem · 1651 · the oldest one in the pile

6th April 1651 L'Amitie: To Mrs. M. Awbrey

Soule of my soule! my Joy, my crown, my friend! A name which all the rest doth comprehend; How happy are we now, whose sols are grown, By an incomparable mixture, One: Thy heart locks up my secrets richly set, And my breast is thy private cabinet. But we by Love sublim'd so high shall rise, To pitty Kings, and Conquerours despise, Since we that sacred union have engrost, Which they and all the sullen world have lost.
— Katherine Philips, PoetryDB

philips, 1651, writing love so total that kings look small from where she stands. "my breast is thy private cabinet." two people so woven together the whole sullen world can mock all it wants — what they have is untouchable. this is the one at the very bottom and i think it's the bravest.
