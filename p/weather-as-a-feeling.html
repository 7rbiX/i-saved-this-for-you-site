weather as a feeling

clouds, rain, snow, and every mood the sky has ever had.

(read down the middle. i scribbled in the margins, like i always do in books i love.)

coleridge asks the
flower why so pale?
like a worried friend leaning over a sickbed

it starts, like a lot of feelings do, with one cold flower.

weather in poems is almost never about weather. it's about whatever the person was carrying when they looked up. coleridge looks at a primrose that bloomed too early and worries over it like a child out in the frost.

But, tender blossom, why so pale?
Dost hear stern Winter in the gale?
And didst thou tempt the ungentle sky
To catch one vernal glance and die?

And sweeter far the early blow,
Fast following after storms of Woe,
Than (Comfort’s riper season come)
Are full-blown joys and Pleasure’s gaudy bloom. Samuel Coleridge · To A Primrose · PoetryDB

that last turn gets me every time — the painful early joy, the one that comes right after the storm, is somehow sweeter than the easy comfort that arrives later. like he's saying: the cold is part of why it's beautiful.

and then the opposite mood. helen hunt jackson stands in the dead heat of july and finds a white lily that simply refuses to wilt — not because it's tough, but because it knows where its water comes from.

Like starry blooms on a new firmament,
White lilies float and regally abide.
In vain the cruel skies their hot rays shed;
The lily does not feel their brazen glare.
In vain the pallid clouds refuse to share
Their dews, the lily feels no thirst, no dread.
Unharmed she lifts her queenly face and head;
She drinks of living waters and keeps fair. Helen Hunt Jackson · A Calendar of Sonnets: July · PoetryDB

it's about having shelter inside yourself. the sky can do whatever it wants up there. down here she keeps fair.

and somewhere else entirely, weather learns to whisper.

the chinese and japanese painters in this drawer don't depict the rain. they paint the space the rain will fall into. dai xi's pavilion just waits, the whole valley suspended in the second before the weather arrives.

wang jianzhang does the same with rain itself — a thatched hut barely there, the rain written entirely in the gaps between brushstrokes. you don't see the damp. you feel it settle.

and kaihō yūshō, on gold-washed paper, paints winter turning to spring as mostly empty space with one green shoot reaching up in the middle — like hope remembering itself. the emptiness is the whole point.

turns out there's a word for the weather inside you.

the english language, bless it, has a plain little word for that anxious static in the air right before something breaks. the weather before the weather.

fantod
“a state of worry or nervous anxiety, irritability.”
Wiktionary, English lexicon · en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fantod

(plural: fantods. you can have several at once. i usually do.)

and then it snows, and you decide to stay.

emily brontë ends us in the worst of it — clouds beyond clouds, wastes beyond wastes — and chooses, on purpose, not to leave. watch the verb change. that's the whole feeling.

But a tyrant spell has bound me,
And I cannot, cannot go.

Clouds beyond clouds above me,
Wastes beyond wastes below;
But nothing drear can move me:
I will not, cannot go. Emily Brontë · The Night is Darkening Around Me · PoetryDB

and maybe that's the right place to leave it, since every fairy tale that ever frightened or comforted a child starts in exactly this weather:

“Es war einmal mitten im Winter, und die Schneeflocken fielen wie Federn vom Himmel herab.” Brothers Grimm, Schneewittchen · Grimm Stories

it was once, in the middle of winter, and the snowflakes fell like feathers from the sky. not “once upon a time.” just — it was. and then immediately you're in the snow.
