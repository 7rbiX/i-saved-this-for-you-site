Colophon, & a Word on How This Was Made

in which the book explains itself

A colophon is the note a printer leaves at the end of a book, telling you who set the type and where, and sometimes saying a small prayer of thanks that the work is finished. This is ours.

The Commonplace Book is set in IM Fell English, a face revived from types cut for the University of Oxford around 1690 by a Dutch punchcutter whose name is half-remembered. The black-letter of the masthead is UnifrakturMaguntia; the notes pinned in the margins are in a passing hand called Pinyon; the little typewritten labels are Special Elite. We chose old letters on purpose. They have carried a great deal of human feeling already, and they did not complain about carrying a little more.

Every plate in this book is a real thing — a painting, a pot, a photograph, a printed leaf — and every one of them is in the public domain, which is to say it belongs to all of us now. Most come from the Open Access collection of The Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, which has done the generous and unfashionable thing of giving its pictures back to the people. We are grateful. Where a maker’s name survived, we have kept it under the picture. Where it did not, we wrote unknown maker, which is the truest and most common signature in the whole history of art.

Most beautiful things were made by people whose names we will never know. This book is for them.

The words were written by a great many hands and then read back by other hands, who argued gently and crossed things out. We have tried, above everything, to keep a human voice — to write the way a person talks across a kitchen table, late, when the dishes are done and there is still tea in the pot. If we have managed it even a little, the credit is not ours alone. It belongs to everyone who ever kept a recipe in the back of a book, or taught a child a song, or said: here, let me show you how this is done.
