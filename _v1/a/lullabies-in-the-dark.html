an incantation

Lullabies in the Dark

what a mother sings, what the child hears

kept by Elen, who rocked the cradle

There is a moment, late, when the house holds its breath. A child is fretful—overtired, fever-warm, the day still lodged in their small bones. A woman (it may be a mother, a grandmother, an aunt, a nurse) sits down and begins to rock. And then, almost without thinking, she sings. Not a song, really. Not something with a shape or an ending or words that matter if you listen for meaning. Something older. A sound that says: I am here. The dark is held. You are safe.

A lullaby is not music for the ear. It is music for the body. It works through repetition—the same phrases returning, the same rise and fall of melody, hypnotic as a heartbeat or the creak of a chair. The rhythm is the point. The warmth of the voice is the point. A lullaby tells the child nothing true about the world and everything true about being held while the world fades away.

The Small Magic

Listen to a lullaby and you hear something strange: it is often minor, sometimes mournful. Many of the old ones carry a note of sorrow, even of warning. Hush now. Sleep. The wolf is far away (but it is out there). The words sometimes speak of danger or loss—a recognition, perhaps, that this cradled moment is temporary, that the world beyond the dark is not safe, that sleep is a small forgetting. But the voice, rocking, says otherwise: You are here. I have got you. Sleep.

Lullaby, and good night, with roses bedight.from the Brahms lullaby, itself built on older folk words

A lullaby is not a song—it is an incantation that says: the dark is not empty, because you are held in it.

This is why they haunt us. A lullaby does not ask to be remembered. It works its way into the body first—into the breath, into the heartbeat—and only much later into the mind. You may not recall the words to a lullaby your grandmother sang to you fifty years ago, but your body knows it. Your hands know how to rock. Your voice knows how to fall into that particular, patient tenderness that is not love alone but something like care taken at the edge of sleep. And sometimes, without warning—a child’s fever in the night, your own child’s small fist, a moment of helplessness—the lullaby returns, not from memory but from the depths of your own being, and you find yourself singing it.

The oldest lullabies may be wordless. The oldest may simply be the mother’s voice, shaped and reshaped, generation into generation, a sound that says what no words need to: rest now. I am here. The dark holds you. You are safe.

A lullaby is not a song—it is an incantation that says: the dark is not empty, because you are held in it.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. It is said that lullabies across cultures share certain musical features—simpler melodies, lower pitches, slower tempos—as if the human voice knows, without teaching, what the drowsy ear needs to hear.
