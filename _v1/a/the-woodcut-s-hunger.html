The Woodcut’s Hunger

carving the world backwards into blocks of wood

kept by Erasmus, who worked the press

The knife goes in backwards. That is the first thing you feel in your hands—the knife, the wood grain rough under your fingers, and the knowledge that the image you carry in your head must be mirror-written, reversed, so that when the ink rolls across it and the paper comes down, the world swings right again. A woodcut artist spends half her attention watching what is not there, the negative space, the air that will print white. It is like thinking in reverse, like writing with your other hand, like dreaming in the language of absence.

The hunger in it—that is the real magic. The woodblock wants to be cut. The artist wants to carve. It is not a gentle thing; it is a conversation between will and resistance, between the vein of the wood and the sharpness of the tool. You feel it in your wrist after hours. The block comes from a particular tree, and you are wrestling with the memory of that tree—its years, its seasons, its patterns of growth—now compressed into something dense enough to hold a thousand printings.

What remains after taking away

Carving a woodcut teaches you something true about the world: what is revealed is revealed by subtraction. The image does not swim up from addition; it emerges by taking away, line by line, the wood that was always there. The black lines that print dark are the wood that survives; the white is the absence, the emptiness, the cut. There is a loneliness in every woodcut—the loneliness of what was chosen to remain.

The woodcut is an old democracy. A peasant could own the image; a merchant could print it a thousand times; a child could trace it into a letter home.a saying

To carve backwards is to love what will be forgotten—the negative space, the cut shavings, the labour that vanishes into the final print.

When the ink first touches the block—that moment just before the paper descends—the carver knows what she has made. No hiding, no second chances. The wood is honest. A rushed cut shows; a hesitation shows; a hand that did not know the knife shows. But so does patience. So does the slight trembling of someone working in candlelight. The print is a record of the carver’s breath, her speed, her heart.

To carve backwards is to love what will be forgotten—the negative space, the cut shavings, the labour that vanishes into the final print.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The best woodblocks come from fruitwood—apple, pear—fine-grained and kind to the knife. There is something tender in that.
