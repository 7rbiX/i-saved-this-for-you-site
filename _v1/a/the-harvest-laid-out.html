a threshold crossed

The Harvest Laid Out

when the year’s work becomes a meal

kept by Thomas, who keeps the orchard ledger

There is a moment each year—late August, early September—when the tables cannot hold any more. Apples still warm from the sun. Tomatoes that smell like earth and rain. Beans spilling from baskets, their pods still stiff and green. You stand in the kitchen, and the air itself seems to have weight. This is the abundance that justifies the months of smaller meals, the careful watching, the thin times.

A harvest is not abstract. It is the particular sweetness of this year’s peaches, not last year’s. It is the way the light falls through the kitchen window onto a heap of squash. It is the sound your knife makes on the cutting board when you are working so steadily that your hands know where to go without asking your mind. My grandmother would begin at dawn. By noon there would be jars lined up, filled with whatever the garden had demanded we preserve—jam, stock, pickled cucumbers in their brine. She worked not because she had to, but because it was the only honest way to say thank you.

The year made visible

To lay out a harvest is to lay out proof. Here is what the rain gave. Here is what the sun paid for. Here is what your hands earned. When you cook from it—when you make a stew or a pie or simply set out bread and cheese and tomato on a plate—you are eating time made edible. You are eating the spring frost that almost killed the fruit trees, the dry spell you nursed through with the hose, the June when everything was green at once.

A shared meal made from the year’s own work is how a people know they are home.

One cannot think well, love well, sleep well, if one has not dined well.a wisdom spoken in many kitchens

There is a generosity in abundance that is different from wealth. You cannot buy a harvest the way it feels when it is yours—when you grew it, or your neighbor grew it and brought it to your door, or you stood in the market among other people all searching for the best of what this season holds. The table heavy with what we together have pulled from the earth carries a blessing that money cannot purchase. It says: we have eaten. We will be fed. We belong to this place.

A shared meal made from the year’s own work is how a people know they are home.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The rhythm of preservation—canning, drying, storing—is also a kind of calendar. Each jar is a date marked in your own hand.
