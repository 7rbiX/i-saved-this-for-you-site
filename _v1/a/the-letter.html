The Parlour ☞ The Letter Desk

the tender risk

The Letter

words folded and sealed for one other person

kept by old Thacker, who knows the post-routes

The weight of a sealed envelope in your hand is nothing—a few grams of paper, a dab of wax—and yet it holds everything you dared not say aloud. You have written it in the silence of your room, alone with the sound of the pen scratching. You have addressed it to a single person. You have folded it. You have pressed your thumb into the wax.

This is not writing for a room full of readers, or for posterity, or for strangers who will judge you from a distance. This is writing for one face you know. One pair of eyes. One heart that may break or mend depending on what you have written. You have made yourself small and particular—not grand, not universal, just yourself, speaking to them.

The Risk of Being Known

A letter is a thing that can be kept or thrown away. It can be read a hundred times, each time finding new meaning. It can be shown to others, or hidden away in a drawer and found after you are gone. Once you seal it and let it go, it is out of your hands. You have sent your voice into the world with no armour. There is no preface saying what you meant, no chance to take back the word that landed wrong.

The letter does not return to us the way speech does. It goes out into silence.a saying

To write a letter is to believe that one other person matters more than safety.

And yet people have always done this. They have written by candlelight, by lamplight, in kitchens and ships and corners of war. They have written to lovers they would not see for years, to children they held before they sailed away, to friends they quarreled with and needed to reconcile. The letter is the voice of longing—longing to be understood, to be forgiven, to reach across distance and say I am still here, I am thinking of you. That is why people kept them. That is why they kept them long after the writer was dust.

To write a letter is to believe that one other person matters more than safety.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. Sometimes a letter was never sent—it sat in a drawer, a conversation the writer had only with themselves, yet addressed to another. In that case it was both letter and diary at once.
  2. The envelope matters. Cheap paper, fine paper, the name written in careful or careless hand—it all speaks before the seal is broken.
