a season of permission

Carnival: When the World Turns Inside Out

the gift and the permission to be otherwise

kept by Lucian, who keeps the costumes

There is a moment when you tie the mask — when your ordinary face vanishes behind silk and papier-mâché, and something cracks open. A woman who has curtsied all her life suddenly moves like a crow, all elbows and laughter. A boy who speaks only when spoken to finds his voice blooming wild. The world watches and says: yes, of course, you were always this.

Carnival is the season when permission becomes visible. For those weeks, the rules soften like wax in sunlight. The kitchen maid can wear the lady’s jewels — not stolen, given. The nobleman must bow to the beggar. The silent become loud, the proud become foolish, and everyone pretends it is the costume’s fault, not theirs. But we know better.

The Mask as Mirror

What a mask reveals is not escape — it is truth with permission. Under the painted bird-face, the severe merchant dances. Under the golden sun-mask, the widow tells jokes that would make her priest blush. These are not new people. These are people as they might have been, or might yet be, if the world had asked differently. The mask gives permission, yes, but permission for something that was always there, waiting.

We are more ourselves in disguise than we have ever been.a saying, old and countless times true

Carnival teaches that the face you were born with is not your only face.

When the season ends and the masks come down, something lingers. You have seen your neighbour’s heart. You have felt what it is to move through the world unrecognised, unbound. And this knowledge — that the self is not fixed, that permission can reshape a person — it does not simply vanish with the mask. It lives in the bone. Come next year, you will tie the cord with your fingers shaking, because you remember. You remember who you became. And the watching crowd will see you, and they will know: you are learning the old magic. You are becoming.

Carnival teaches that the face you were born with is not your only face.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The carnival season — Shrovetide in some lands, Carnaval elsewhere — was not frivolous permission but held knowledge: that a person rigidly shaped by circumstance might remember, for a little time, what other shapes are possible.
