The Kite and the Wind

what a child holds that can never be caught

kept by Thomas Wickham, who flies paper in the high fields

The string goes taut under your thumb. That tug—it is the only honest thing you will ever hold. A kite is not a toy you possess, not the way you possess a marble or a doll. It is something alive in your hands, answering to the wind, to the air currents you cannot see. You feel the breath of the world through a thread no thicker than your sorrow.

A child at six or seven with a kite learns something without being told: that you can hold something and let it go at once. The paradox is the whole point. You run, the paper diamond climbing into the blue. Your hands stay down. The sky keeps what you released. And this is not loss. This is the first true joy, because it asks you to trust something you cannot hold.

The Grammar of String

In Bengal they make kites of bamboo and paper no thicker than a breath. In the markets of Kabul, in the hills above Mexican valleys, in fishing villages where the wind comes off the water steady and salt—children have always run toward emptiness holding paper and string. The shape changes. The bamboo is crooked or true. But the lesson is the same: to fly something is to let it go far enough to breathe.

He who would see the wind must watch the kite dance.a saying of the kite fliers

The kite teaches the hand to let go before the heart is ready.

There is a moment when the wind is steady and the string goes out and out, and you are no longer pulling. The kite is pulling you—your arm aches, your neck cranes back. You are a small anchor for something that wants the sky. The fear comes and goes. Will it snap? Will it fall? And then comes the knowledge: it will do what it will do. Your only job is to feel the tug, to stay present for the moment it is yours, and then, when evening comes, to bring it home. A child learns all the mathematics of holding on and letting go from one afternoon of wind and paper. This is why every civilization has made them. This is why we have not forgotten.

The kite teaches the hand to let go before the heart is ready.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. Some kites are never brought home—lost in the branches, snagged on a roof. These are not failures. These are the ones that learned to fly.
