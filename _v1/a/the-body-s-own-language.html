The Parlour ☞ The Dance Floor

a gesture made clear

The Body’s Own Language

what dance says that words cannot

kept by old Merrow, who teaches in the village hall

There is a moment in every turn where the dancer becomes the turn itself—where intention and muscle and the earth all speak the same word at once. Watch it happen: a woman pivots on one foot, the other leg extending, and in that fraction of a second something true about her passes through the air. Not happiness exactly. Not sorrow. But something the body knows that the mouth has no word for.

I have watched this for forty years in a cold hall with a sprung wooden floor and light coming sideways through tall windows. The same turn, done by a hundred different spines. A girl learning, her shoulders tight with concentration. A man, years later, doing it with the ease of something he has not thought about in a decade. An old woman holding the turn for half a beat longer, as if refusing to let it go. Each one says something different. Each one true.

The geometry of trust

A leap is the body asking a question of the air: will you hold me? And the air answers by holding. But before the leap comes the gathering—a breath taken low, the knees bending, the arms drawing in. This is the body confessing its weight, its doubt, its small hope. Then the push, and the flight, and that moment of impossible weightlessness where for a fraction of a second the dancer and gravity are negotiating.

The body says what words are too shy to speaka saying

In a turn, the body tells the truth about who is afraid and who is free.

You cannot fake the precision of a good turn. You cannot pretend to own your body if you do not. This is why dance is honest in a way that almost nothing else is. You can lie with your mouth. You can lie with your hands. But your feet know. Your spine knows. Your breath knows where you truly are. A good dancer is someone who has stopped lying to their own bones.

The turn passes down, foot to foot, the way a recipe passes or a prayer. Someone shows you. Your body remembers what your mind cannot hold. Years later, you show someone else, and the turn that your grandmother learned becomes part of their spine, their breath. The body holds more than the heart. It holds the hands that came before.

In a turn, the body tells the truth about who is afraid and who is free.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The floor itself is a teacher—a good one learns to listen to it, to feel where it gives, where it is kind.
