The Parlour ☞ The Apothecary

a craft remembered

Steep and Sip

the practice of making and drinking tea

kept by Eleanor, who keeps the kettle on

The water goes in the pot—you hear it before you feel it, that familiar soft roar as it climbs toward heat. While you wait for it to boil, your hands find the dried leaves in their paper or their jar. Chamomile gold as old pollen. Mint so crisp you smell autumn in June. The leaves are small, rough, honest, nothing like the dust in a teabag. You measure by want, not rule: a pinch more if someone is grieving, a pinch less if the day is tender. This is how knowledge lives in hands.

When the water reaches that rolling moment, you pour it onto the leaves and watch what happens—it is never the same twice. The heat releases colour like an old story being remembered. The leaves unfurl. The room fills with a ghost of the plant as it was living: the garden it came from, the sun that sweetened it, the hands that gathered and dried it. You are undoing time. You are bringing the plant back to itself, just enough.

The waiting

Now comes the hardest part—you do not rush it. You sit. A few minutes is not a long time in the body, but in the mind it is an ocean. Your hands rest around the cup and feel the warmth rise into your palms. You watch the colour deepen in the cup. You breathe the steam and let your eyes soften. This is not hurrying toward the next thing. This is an old form of saying to yourself: your body matters, your trouble is seen, your healing has already begun.

The slow cup is the curea saying

To make tea is to say yes to your own keeping

When you finally sip, the liquid is not just water anymore. It carries the plant’s gift, its particular warmth, its medicine both small and large. Some teas are for fever, some for sleep, some for a throat that has wept. But all of them carry one message the same: you are worthy of this time, this care, this small ceremony. You are worth the hot water and the waiting. In a world that asks you to rush, to produce, to move always forward, the tea says stop. Steep. Sip. Let yourself be held.

To make tea is to say yes to your own keeping

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The herbs themselves do most of the work—a good leaf from a good plant teaches you as it opens in the water. Trust what you smell and taste. Your body knows what it needs.
