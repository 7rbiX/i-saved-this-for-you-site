A Room of One’s Own

shelter, refuge, the privacy that changes a life

kept by Josephine, who mends the windows

A door with a lock on the inside. Not grand—just a wooden frame, a knob worn smooth by hands, the kind of latch that catches with a soft click. For most of human time, you did not have this. You lived in the open press of others: the family bed, the shared hall, the eyes of the village on everything you did. Then, if you were lucky or became lucky, there came a room. A threshold you could cross alone.

It is not luxury that matters here, though some rooms are lovely. It is the arithmetic of solitude. A place where you do not have to account for yourself. Where you can think without audience, grieve without performance, sit in the ordinary clothes of your mind. You can close the door and the world outside becomes the world outside. What happens in this room—the shape of your morning, the tears, the small rebellion, the book read by one thin candle—belongs only to you.

The privacy that changes a life

For centuries, a room of one’s own was a luxury that came with a price: you had to be born to it, or marry into it, or never have one at all. Women especially knew this. A shared bed in a shared room; the perpetual presence of others. Then came the revolution of small means—the boarding house, the apartment, the rented room in the city. Servants and factory workers and clerks saved their wages to rent a corner that was theirs alone. It was not comfort they were buying. It was themselves.

The mind must have a room of its own if it is to think.not a word-for-word quote, but the old knowing

A door that closes is the first room of freedom.

The dignity of it is quiet. You need not decorate it for anyone. It need not be large. A cot, a shelf, a window to look from. Some people put a plant there; some keep it sparse as a cell. The point is the door. The point is the threshold. Once you have known this kind of silence—the silence of being alone by choice, not by exile—you understand something about what it means to be yourself. The world makes its demands. The door reminds you that you are separate from it. In that separation lives a kind of wholeness.

A door that closes is the first room of freedom.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. Many of the oldest houses had no separate rooms at all—just one hall where everyone lived, worked, slept, cooked. Privacy is a newcomer to dwelling, and still not everyone has it. That makes it more precious, not less.
