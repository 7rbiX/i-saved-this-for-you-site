a held breath

The Moment Before Music

why people gather to move together

kept by Clara, who minds the music box

The room is not yet full of sound. In this second before the first note, you can hear your own breath, the shuffle of feet on boards, someone’s hand smoothing the front of their dress. The air tastes like expectation. There is sweat on palms that have not yet moved, and the small ache of muscles braced for something—but what?

This is the oldest longing, I think. Not for the music itself, but for the permission it brings. When the music starts, the rules change. The body which was bound by sense, by shyness, by the simple weight of being alone in yourself—suddenly it is released. What was impossible becomes inevitable. A stranger’s hand becomes safe. Your feet remember things you never learned.

The gathering

We come to the dance floor because we are lonely, each of us, in ways we cannot name. Not sad—but separate. Locked in our own skin. The moment before music is when we are still private, still ourselves, but beginning to sense the others: their breathing, their waiting, the shape of their wanting. It is tender and half-wild.

In the pause before the measure starts, the dancer and the dance are still becoming one.a saying

We gather to be together in a way that words have never allowed.

When the music comes—when the violin wakes or the drum sounds or the old familiar tune begins—it is not really the music that changes us. It is the permission we have been waiting for, the signal that says: now you are not alone; now your body is allowed to speak; now you belong to this room, this moment, these feet beside your own. The moment before music is where hope lives. It is where we decide, wordlessly, to be brave.

We gather to be together in a way that words have never allowed.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The waiting matters as much as the dancing. A good musician knows this.
