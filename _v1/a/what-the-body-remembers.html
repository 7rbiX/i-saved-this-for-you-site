an experience held in the skin

What the Body Remembers

the feeling of being tended, the long reaching toward wellness

kept by Thomas Wrey, who keeps a fire in the back room

A hand touches your forehead. It is cool—that is the first thing you notice, before you even know you are being touched. Cool and deliberate, the way a person’s hand is when they are paying attention to you, not just passing through the room. This is the oldest medicine. Before the jar on the shelf, before the name of the herb, before anyone knew why it worked, there was this: someone noticing you were burning. Someone deciding to do something about it.

The body does not forget. It stores up the small mercies. Years later you will feel the same coolness in a drink held out to you on a hot road, and something in your ribs will settle. You will know, without being told, that you are safe. This is what tending does—it teaches the flesh to trust again.

The Slow Work of Care

Being healed is not a hurried thing, though we live as if it must be. The person who knows this sits beside you—not above you, not rushing off to the next room. They have time. They have made time. You can feel the difference in how they move, in how they listen to what your body is trying to say. They might steep you a tea, might hold a cloth to your temple. Might simply sit, and by sitting, show you that you matter enough to stop for.

The smallest acts of attention remake us.

Heal me, and I shall be healed; save me, and I shall be saved.the old prayer, from many lips

When someone tends you—truly tends you, with hands that have practiced kindness—you become a little more whole just from being seen. The herb helps. The jar on the shelf holds real knowledge. But what remakes us most is knowing we are worth the keeping. That someone looked at our breaking and did not turn away, but reached for the right small thing to help. We carry that forward. In our own hands, when it is our turn to hold the jar and offer the warm cup to someone else who is burning.

The smallest acts of attention remake us.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The hands that tend us teach our bodies to remember safety. We spend the rest of our lives reaching for that feeling.
