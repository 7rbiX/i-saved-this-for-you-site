The Parlour ☞ The Toy Box

an alchemy of childhood

The Stick and the Gift

what a child makes from nothing

kept by Thomas, who gathers drift-wood

A stick is never just a stick in a child’s hand. It is warm from the palm. It has weight and balance—light enough to swing, substantial enough to mean something. The bark still holds its shape, rough under the thumb. Pick it up and you own it, not because you purchased it but because you saw what it could be before it became that thing.

This is the deepest magic of childhood: the refusal to see a thing as finished. A branch becomes a sword, and the child’s arm knows how to wield it. A twig becomes a wand, and suddenly the world obeys other laws. A long stick becomes a horse—all clattering gallops and imagined breath. The gift is not in the object but in the eye that transforms it. The child does not ask permission. She does not wait for the factory or the shop. She simply sees.

The poverty of plenty

We tell ourselves we are giving our children gifts by filling their rooms with toys—the plastic sword that comes in a box, the talking doll, the approved and tested thing. But the child who has everything sometimes has less, because the stick from the wood teaches something the purchased thing cannot. It teaches invention. It teaches that the world will answer your hunger if you speak to it plain. A stick is a question mark. A toy is a period.

What is imagination but the liberty to make something where nothing stood?a saying

A child with a stick and the freedom to use it knows he is not small—he knows he can change the world.

The stick is honest. It will not pretend to be more than itself. It will splinter if you break it. It will be wet after rain and dry by noon. It teaches the body how to move with purpose. It teaches limits and possibility in the same grip. And when the child is done—when she has been a queen and a knight and a builder and a general—she can leave it where it was, and it remains a stick, faithful, ready for the next hand.

A child with a stick and the freedom to use it knows he is not small.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The finest toys are often the ones we do not notice making—until we see them working in a child’s hands and understand, all at once, what we have done.
