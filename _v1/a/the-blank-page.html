The Blank Page

the terror and mercy of beginning

kept by Esther, who kept journals in the attic

The pen sits on the wood. You have set it there, careful as placing a bird. The page in front of you is so white it almost hums. Not a mark on it. Not a single word to argue with, to push against, to build from. You have opened the notebook with intention—you even sat down at a particular hour, the light coming in a certain way—but now the whiteness is speaking, and it is louder than you are.

Everyone who writes knows this moment. The paralysis before the first word. It is not writer’s block, not entirely. It is something closer to fear. The blank page is a space of infinite possibility, which is another way of saying infinite choice, which is another way of saying you might choose wrongly. You might write something that is small, or false, or ugly. You might waste this pristine thing. So you wait. You sharpen the pencil again. You adjust the chair. You look at the page.

The Courage That Picks Up the Pen

But here is the mercy hidden inside the terror: everyone else who ever wrote started exactly here. The blank page is not a problem to solve. It is the place where writing happens. Not despite the blankness, but because of it. That whiteness is not a judgment; it is an invitation.

You do not have to see the whole staircase, only the first step.a saying

The courage to begin is not the courage of heroes. It is small and daily and ridiculous. It is the act of putting the pen down on the white and letting it make a mark—any mark—and then another. You will write something inadequate. You will cross it out. That is not failure. That is the work itself. Every page that matters, every letter, every journal, every scribbled note that someone kept and found years later—all of it began here, with someone deciding that an imperfect word was better than the perfect silence.

The blank page is not waiting to be perfect. It is waiting for you to be brave enough to ruin it.

Pick up the pen. The first word is always the smallest one. After that, the way opens.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The truly brave thing is not the writing itself, but the writing badly, with no one watching. The page does not care. Only you do. And you, dear keeper, are kinder than you think.
