a bell-tower's gift

The Cathedral Clock

time given to a village, measurable and kind

kept by Theo Merriweather, keeper of the tower stairs

There is a bell that has rung the hour in the same stone tower for three hundred and seventeen years. It is not grand—not the famous bells of cities, not a thing that draws people to come and stare up. It is simply there, part of the air, part of what the village knows. The clock face itself is the colour of cream, spotted with rust where the rain finds its way in, and the numerals are Roman, worn smooth by wind. When you stand below and look up at it, you understand that time is not invisible. Time has a face. Time is a thing you can see.

Before that clock, the village kept time as bodies do: by the light, by hunger, by the feel of seasons turning. The baker knew when to start the fire because that was when the baker always started it. The fields were turned at the right time because that was the remembered time. But a clock that anyone could read, that anyone could hear—that changed the whole shape of living. The church bell told you when the working day began, when you were late, when to come to prayer. It made you part of a crowd moving to the same drum.

What a machine can do for a place

The clockmaker who installed it is long forgotten. He came, climbed the stairs with his heavy case of tools, worked in the narrow stone room where the mechanism lives, and left. Probably he never knew that his work would shape nearly four centuries of mornings. The clock itself is not complicated by modern standards—an escapement, weights on chains, the bells hung in the tower above. But in its simplicity is its genius. Simple things last. Simple things you can mend with patience and oil and a little prayer.

A village is bound together not just by love but by shared time.

Every hour that rings is an hour that was, an hour that is, an hour that will be—all at once.an old sexton, recorded

The clock stopped once, in 1943, when the mechanism jammed and no one could be spared from the war to climb and fix it. For three months, the village heard only silence where the bells had been. People said they felt unmade. They said time moved strangely without a voice to measure it. When it ran again, there was such quiet joy that the bells seemed to ring louder than they ever had. Some who were children then—they are old now—they still tear up when they hear it. Not from sentimentality. From a deep knowing that a thing which marks your hours, which is the same every day of your life, becomes part of your skeleton.

The clock still chimes. It is maintained now by a woman from three villages over who drives out once a year, winds the weights, cleans the escapement, and listens for what it needs. There are fewer such clocks kept living each year. Most tick silently in museums.

It is strange to think of time as a gift. But that is what a cathedral clock is. Not the measurement of time—clocks did not invent that. The gift is the gathering. The bell says: we are here together, marking the same moment, bound by the same brass and stone and sky. And that, for a place, is everything.

A village is bound together not just by love but by shared time.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The keeper of such a clock must learn something about patience that no book can teach—the way a machine breathes, the way it speaks, the way it asks for kindness.
