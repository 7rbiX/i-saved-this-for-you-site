The Grandmother’s Cats

companionship in the small hours

kept by Theo, who keeps the house door open

A cat sits on the back of an old armchair where the light pools in the afternoon. She does not look at you, but she sits there. This is the moment you understand: she chose this. Not the house, exactly. This room. This chair. The particular angle of sun on fabric worn thin by decades of shoulders and heads. The small heat of a woman who had no one left to keep but the creatures who stayed.

There is something about a cat in a house where someone lives alone that settles the weight of silence into something almost like company. The cat does not demand. Does not need you to be cheerful or able or young. She simply knows the rhythm of the small hours — the 3 a.m. when sleep will not come, when the mind goes over things, when a hand reaches out in the dark and feels warm fur, feels breath, feels the presence of another living thing that asks nothing but to be there.

What the cat knows

A cat remembers the shape of your hand before it remembers your face. She knows which floorboard creaks, which door opens to the garden, which cupboard holds the promise of small fish. But more than this — she knows the house the way a person knows it in their bones. She knows where it is warm. Where it is quiet. Where a human sits for hours and does not move. She chooses these places. She chooses to stay.

The cat is a woman in furs, as someone once said.a saying

We do not save the cat. The cat saves us by deciding we are worth the keeping.

There is a particular kind of love that asks nothing back. A grandmother, alone in a house at night, can touch the soft head of a cat and know that she is not quite alone. The cat does not care if she is sad. Does not need her to be useful anymore. Does not leave. And so the woman lets herself be vulnerable in a way she would never allow another human to see — lets herself cry into that small, listening dark, knowing that the cat will still be there in the morning, waiting by the empty bowl, alive and particular and stubbornly, tenderly present.

We do not save the cat. The cat saves us by deciding we are worth the keeping.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. There are houses where the cats come in from the rain and never leave. These are not accidents.
