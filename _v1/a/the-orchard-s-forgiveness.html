The Parlour ☞ The Garden Gate

a meditation on tending

The Orchard’s Forgiveness

living trees and the gift of continuity

kept by Thomas, who keeps the old apple trees

There is a particular quiet that falls over an orchard in early morning, before the bees wake. The light moves between the branches like a thing that knows its way. You can smell the soil and something else—the patience of the place. A tree does not rush. It has never rushed. This is not virtue. It is simply how trees are made.

Plant an apple tree and you are making a promise to someone you will not meet. Your hands are in the earth, but your hands are doing the work. You are the small part. The tree is the long part. It might live for fifty years, for a hundred. It will feed your children and their children. You will prune it, and most likely you will do it wrong sometimes, and the tree will forgive you. It will fruit anyway.

On the Question of Return

An orchard asks very little: decent soil, winter, water in the right season, and human hands that show up and know what branches to cut. But it gives back with a generosity that is almost embarrassing. Year after year. Spring blossoms that smell like the beginning of the world. Then the small green weight of apples forming, swelling. The harvest—your hands full, a ladder against bark you have touched a thousand times, the fruit warm from the sun.

The people before us planted knowing they would not taste the fruit.a saying

This is the strange contract of the orchard. You tend what will outlive you. You feed someone you will never know. And this is not sad—it is the only way forward. Your grandmother planted for you. You plant for a child not yet born. The tree stands at the centre of this exchange, utterly indifferent to your gratitude, yet somehow more honest than anything that needs to be thanked.

An orchard is forgiveness made wooden, made living, made patient.

When you lose something—a harvest to frost, a beloved to time—an orchard teaches you to wait. Next spring, the buds will form again. Not the same season. Never the same season. But the same faithful return. This is what an orchard knows that we have to learn: that continuity is not about perfection. It is about showing up again. It is about roots that go down long after the hands above ground have become dust.

An orchard is forgiveness made wooden, made living, made patient.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. Old trees remember. A scar on the bark from a careless pruning forty years back still shows. The tree grew around it.
