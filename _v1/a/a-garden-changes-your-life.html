A Garden Changes Your Life

quiet witnesses to the seasons, to grief, to becoming

kept by H. Moorside, who keeps the plot

There is a moment when someone walks into a garden because they have to, not because they want to. The legs do it. The hands reach for a spade. Nothing has been decided; there is only a small piece of ground that needs turning, and turning the earth is what comes next. What comes next.

The reasons are always different. A marriage ends, and a person stands alone in a house that no longer feels like theirs. Someone loses a child, and the world becomes too loud with absence, so they dig. The diagnosis comes in winter, and come spring they have to do something with their body, something that matters, something their hands can understand. An old grief that would not leave finally finds its place in the dark, wet earth.

What grows when we are broken

The garden does not ask why. It takes the work—the turned soil, the blood from thorns, the shoulders bending—and it asks again tomorrow. There is a strange grace in that. The garden has no opinions about your sorrow. It only needs water and time. And you, needing something that needs you back, discover that you can still do this: you can still make something grow.

To plant a garden is to believe in tomorrow.a saying, old and true

The earth does not know your name, but it knows your hands.

People find different things in the garden. Some find their hands again—they thought they had forgotten how to use them, but a tomato plant teaches them. Some find that their mind, the mind that will not stop spinning, becomes quiet when they kneel. Others discover that grief can live in the soil as well as in the chest, that some of the weight can be given to the dark below. And some—the ones who stay—find that a garden is a place where you do not have to explain. The zinnia blooms. The lettuce grows. You were here. You made this. That is enough.

The change is not magic. It is slower than that, and more real. You do not heal because you planted seeds. But somewhere in the waiting, in the daily small acts of tending, in the dirt under your fingernails, something shifts. Your life does not become simple. But you find yourself standing in the morning light with a basket of beans, and there is still something in you that can be glad.

The earth does not know your name, but it knows your hands.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. Gardens teach the same lesson every spring: that things die and return. That patience is not emptiness but a kind of fullness we have forgotten how to feel.
