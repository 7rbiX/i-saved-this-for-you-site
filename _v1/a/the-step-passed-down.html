an ancient patience

The Step Passed Down

hands and feet teaching hands and feet

kept by Samuel, who counts the measures

Your grandmother’s hand is warm on your shoulder blade. She is not speaking. The music from the radio—old, scratched, a voice singing in a language that carries the weight of morning—has started its third repetition, and you are still getting it wrong. Your feet want to step where your mother steps. Your hips want to move the way your sister moves. But your grandmother’s fingers tap once, twice, on your back, and you understand that you must wait, that the rhythm lives in a different place than you thought, deeper, where the floor is.

This is how steps live from one body to another. Not in watching. In feeling. The hand on your back is a language older than words. When a teacher stands in front of the mirror with a student—arms up, chin steady, spine tall—something is passing that no instruction manual could hold. The corrections are small: a gentle pressure on the hip, a lift of the elbow, a nod that says yes, that. That breath. That alignment. That surrender to gravity and grace at once.

The Gift of Repetition

You do the same sequence forty times. Fifty times. Your feet ache. Your mind wants to wander. But something is happening in the repetition—the step is becoming yours. Not a copy. Not a mimicry. It has lived in someone’s body before you, and now it is living in yours, with your particular weight, your particular speed, your particular soul inside it. This is how tradition moves. Not as a relic in a museum case, but as a living thing, breathing, changing shape with each hand that passes it on.

Watch me now. Not with your eyes. With your feet.a saying from the dancers

The step does not belong to any one person—it belongs to the space between them, the generosity of showing, the humility of learning.

There is a tenderness in this that the world does not always honour. A teacher who takes the time to show a student, body to body, is giving something that cannot be rushed. They are offering their own knowledge, earned through years of sweat and failure. And the student, in standing still enough to feel, is offering back a kind of faith: I will let your body teach mine. I will trust you enough to be vulnerable. I will carry this forward.

You are learning a step that may outlive you. Somewhere, someday, someone else will stand where you are standing now, confused, aching, reaching toward something they cannot quite grasp—and a hand will be placed on their back, and they will feel it: the warmth of all the people who came before.

The step does not belong to any one person—it belongs to the space between them.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The oldest dances have no beginning, no one who invented them. They came from the needs of the body, and from the people who loved enough to pass them on.
