The Act of Keeping

why we copy things by hand into empty books

kept by Margot, keeper of the ledgers

A pencil, a clean page, and the decision that something matters enough to write down by hand. Not typed, not screenshotted, not saved to the cloud with ten thousand other fragments. Written. Your own hand making the marks, your own time spent, your own choosing to say: this poem, this recipe, this sentence someone said in a kitchen—this will not be lost on my watch.

The commonplace book is the opposite of scrolling. It is the opposite of the algorithm that decides what you see tomorrow. It is you, alone in an evening, with a pen, making a small act of rebellion: I choose. I decide what lives in my house, in my hands, in the hours that are mine. Nothing is here that I did not place here.

what the hand knows that the thumb does not

When you write something by hand, you do not skim it. The rhythm of your arm, the weight of the pen, the space it takes on the page—these slow you. You read it as you write it. You live with it a moment longer. By the time your pen lifts, something has moved from the outside world into your memory, into your body’s knowledge. It has become yours in a different way than clicking ‘save’ ever could.

We are the only animal that keeps its past written in another animal’s skin. That is what it means to be human.Margaret Atwood

To copy something by hand is to say: I trust this enough to spend my time on it. I trust you enough to give you my hours.

In a world that forgets by default, the commonplace book is an act of keeping faith. Not with the past, exactly—but with the future. With someone who will open this book when you cannot. With the understanding that what we choose to save says everything about what we believe is worth living for.

To copy something by hand is to say: I trust this enough to spend my time on it.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The earliest surviving commonplace books date to the medieval monastery, where monks copied prayers and wisdom onto vellum. But the practice truly bloomed in the Renaissance, when paper became affordable and silence became a luxury that ordinary people could afford, one evening at a time.
  2. If you have never kept one: buy a notebook that fits in your hand. No pressure. No system. Just start.
