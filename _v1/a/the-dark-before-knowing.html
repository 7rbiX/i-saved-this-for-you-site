The Dark Before Knowing

why we need stories, what they do in the soul

kept by Thomas Wicke, lampmaker's apprentice and keeper of night-tales

A child in the dark does not want to be told everything will be fine. She wants Hansel and Gretel lost deeper in the forest, their fear made real and held, and then—only then—the way out. The fear must be true first.

We come to stories not because life is too hard but because life is too real, too close, too much our own small thing. A tale lets you touch something without it touching you back—not yet. You can hold the witch’s hunger, the youngest son’s nothing-to-lose, the curse that poisons kingdoms. You can live inside them as if they were carved from soap, solid enough to grip, light enough to set down.

The real hungers

The hunger goes three ways. First: we want to be changed—to know it is possible. A peasant girl becomes a queen not because peasants ever do, but because something in her, and therefore in us, recognizes that a soul is not fixed by the cradle it lay in. Second: we want justice that is not the world’s justice—we want the cruel stepmother to face the measure of her cruelty, not to live comfortable and die in her bed. Third: we want to belong to something larger than the house we were born in. A story makes a circle and we sit inside it, and we are no longer alone in the dark.

The tale is the oldest law we keep; it tells us we are not the first to be afraid, and we will not be the last.a saying

A story is a safe place to be remade.

The grandmother knows this when she whispers the tale to the child in the dark. She is not soothing; she is handing over a stone—heavy, true, older than both of them—and saying: here is what it feels like to be lost, to be small, to want something that seems impossible. Here is what it feels like to endure. And here, listen—here is the turn. The way home. Not easy. But real.

A story is a safe place to be remade.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The dark before knowing is different from darkness itself—it is the dark of not-yet-knowing, the child awake and waiting, before the tale begins and after it ends.
