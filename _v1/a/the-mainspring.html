The Mainspring

what keeps time beating in the dark

kept by Thomas, who tends the regulator clock

It lies coiled inside the barrel like a sleeping thing—a ribbon of tempered steel, no thicker than your thumbnail, wound so tight it seems patient beyond measure. You cannot see it working. That is its gift. Pick up a pocket watch that has been running for a hundred years and you have the mainspring to thank; the whole conversation between past and future, the steady pulse of a thousand heartbeats a day, lives in that single spiral of metal.

The spring wants to straighten. That is its nature—steel remembers, always, that it was meant to be flat and free. But the watchmaker winds it into a curve when the watch is born, twisting it tighter and tighter until it holds a kind of potential, a stored anger that it will spend the rest of its life unspooling, grain by grain, second by second. This is not violence. It is patience. A mainspring understands that power given slowly is power that lasts.

The Crown and the Coil

You wind the watch by turning the crown—that little knob on the case—and you feel the mainspring gather itself tighter, feel the resistance. You are loading the future into a piece of metal. The spring has a natural limit; wind it past that point and something breaks. A watchmaker knows this by touch. Their fingers learn to feel the moment when the spring is full, the same way a baker knows when bread dough has been worked enough.

The spring is the clock’s confession that time must be paid for.a saying among timekeepers

What else in the world does so much work and asks only to be let alone?

A mainspring fails from fatigue—the metal grows tired and loses its spring, or it snaps without warning, a small sound like a held breath released. When that happens, the clock stops. But here is the beauty: the spring can be made new. A repairer can unwind it, straighten it, temper it again in heat, and wind it fresh. The coil that has lost its memory can learn again. This is why old clocks feel sacred; they have been given back their heartbeat more than once, and they are grateful in the only way a thing of metal can be—by keeping time, by keeping faith, by never stopping until you ask them to.

What else in the world does so much work and asks only to be let alone?

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The mainspring in a large clock—a cathedral regulator, say—can be the width of a man’s hand and rolled into a barrel the size of a teapot. The springs in the smallest music-box watches are so fine you would lose one in a moment of carelessness.
