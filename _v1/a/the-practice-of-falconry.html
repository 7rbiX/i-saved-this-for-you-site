The Practice of Falconry

the art of letting go and calling back

kept by H. Aldwyn, who kept the mews

A hawk will sit on your gloved fist, and you will feel its talons grip through leather, the grip of a creature that could pierce your flesh and will not. This is the first lesson: trust moves both ways. The bird came to you by hunger and by habit, by the small bells sewn to its legs that call you when it hunts, by the leather jesses — the straps looped around its legs — that tether it not through cruelty but through love. A falconer does not own a hawk the way a man owns a horse. He keeps it, the way you keep a secret.

The apprenticeship is long. In the old books, a young person would serve a master falconer for years, learning to read the bird’s moods the way a sailor reads the sea — the flatten of feathers that means fear, the ruffled stand that means eagerness, the tilt of the head that means hunger. You learn what the hawk will eat and when, how to bathe it, how to hold the jesses so they do not burn its legs. You learn the casting — the clean throw of your arm that launches the bird into the sky, the moment your fingers open and release. Most of all, you learn to wait.

The Voice

There is a call the falconer makes when the hawk is far and high, lost in the blue. It is not language — it is the sound of home, of hunger satisfied, of a place to rest. The hawk hears it and turns. It folds its wings and falls through the air towards your fist, towards the lure, towards you. And you must trust, in that moment, that it will come. That the bond you have built — one day at a time, one patient feeding, one released flight and returned hunt — holds true. A falconer who loses faith loses the hawk.

You cannot love a hawk into staying. You can only love it into returning.

Hawk by the hand, hawk in the heart, hawk in the sky — all three must know each other.The Book of St. Albans, a medieval falconry text

There is grief in falconry, too. Hawks die. They fly into storms or hunger, they find a mate in the wild and do not come back, or they simply grow old. A falconer grieves the way one grieves for a wild thing that lived in the house for a season — not with the long ache of ownership, but with the sharper, cleaner sadness of something that was always meant to leave. And he begins again, training the next bird, making the same patient fists, throwing the same call into the sky, and waiting. The practice is less about mastery than about the slow, strange art of being worthy of return.

You cannot love a hawk into staying. You can only love it into returning.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The bells worn by hawks — called hawks’ bells — are among the oldest and loveliest of human implements. Some were cast in the 12th century and are still heard ringing in the sky.
  2. Falconry has always been a gentleness made of steel. It shaped kings and monks alike. To keep a hawk is to live with a wild thing that chooses you, day by day.
