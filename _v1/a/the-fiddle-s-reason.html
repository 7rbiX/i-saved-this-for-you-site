the instrument remembered

The Fiddle’s Reason

the violin in folk hands, rough and radiant

kept by Thomas Brennan, who keeps the door at the fiddlers' hall

A worn fiddle in your hands is heavier than it looks. The varnish is thin where two centuries of thumbs have rested. The chin rest is darkened by use. You can feel the slight warping in the wood—not a flaw but a signature, the way the thing has learned to hold itself. When you draw the bow across the strings, your whole chest answers. This is not the instrument of a music room. This is something else.

The fiddle gathers people in a way that other instruments do not. A piano sits in one place, waiting to be approached. A harp demands attention, asks you to watch. But a fiddle—a fiddle walks into a room. It stands in the hand of someone at the corner of a kitchen or the edge of a fire, and the sound it makes goes out like an invitation that doesn’t ask permission. The strings are close to the bow. The air around them trembles. You feel it in your ribs before you know you are listening.

Why this one thing

It is not the fiddle’s size that matters. It is that it is small enough to be owned, portable enough to travel, loud enough to carry a room, and exact enough to break a heart. A man could carry it to a wedding six miles over the hills. A woman could play it while the bread rose. A child could hold it and make the sound of something wider than themselves. There are no barriers between the player and the sound. Your fingers on the strings. Your bow. The sound is yours in a way that a great orchestra is never yours. It is nakedness. It is a voice.

The fiddle does not lie. It will tell you exactly how the player feels in every note.a saying

A fiddle is a place where joy and sorrow take each other’s hands.

At a wedding, a good fiddler makes the moment when strangers become companions. At a crossroads, alone, a fiddler makes the loneliness into something that has a shape. In a stranger’s fingers, the fiddle becomes a small tent you can stand under. The music fills the silence in such a way that the silence is not gone—it is there, held, known, made beautiful. This is what the fiddle does. It takes the things that live in us and gives them air to breathe. It is why, centuries after the last player has turned to dust, we still reach for it.

A fiddle is a place where joy and sorrow take each other’s hands.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The worn fiddles are often the best. The wood has settled into knowing what it is meant to do.
  2. Listen sometime to the sound a fiddle makes in a small room. Notice how the corners of the room answer.
