The Dance Tune

music that moves the feet, that gathers strangers

kept by Thomas, who keeps the old fiddle at the crossroads

The moment before is still. The fiddler stands, bow loose at her side. The floor is a grid of single people, couples, small knots of conversation. The air is thick with waiting. Then her fingers find the strings and the bow drops. Not gently—a reel does not ask permission. It demands. The first notes rise like sparks, and you feel it before you think it: your foot wants to move. Your hip knows what the tune is saying.

A dance tune is not music for listening. It is music for doing. It comes from centuries of people who understood that the body has its own hunger, its own language. A reel, a hornpipe, a jig, a waltz—each one is a conversation between sound and flesh. The musicians play the pattern, but the dancers are the ones who complete it. Without the feet, the tune is only half-alive.

The Floor Fills

Watch what happens in the seconds after that opening phrase. A man steps forward. His partner takes his hand without thinking, moving into him the way water finds its level. Others see it and remember what their bodies know. Suddenly the floor is full—not crowded, but held. Alive. The dancers’ feet strike the boards in unison and fracture again. The music does not slow down. It never does. The tune drives forward like wind through a door, and the dancers chase it, catch it, fall into it.

The feet remember what the mind forgets.a saying

A dance tune knows something about joy that nothing else does: it makes you forget yourself and find yourself at once.

There is something old here, something that goes back further than we can trace. Before there was a stage, before there was an audience, there was a fire and a drum. People moving together in the dark, their bodies speaking to each other through the ground, through the air. A dance tune is that same fire, that same spell, dressed in strings and breath. It gathers strangers and makes them a body. It asks nothing of you but your presence, your willingness to let sound move your bones.

When the tune ends—sudden, final—the dancers stop mid-motion. For a breath, everyone is still again. Then the fiddler laughs, or someone claps, or the next tune begins at once. But those who were dancing carry something with them into the next moment: the memory of moving as one. Of being held by sound. Of being alive inside a song.

A dance tune makes you forget yourself and find yourself at once.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The oldest dance tunes have no single author. They belong to everyone who has ever danced them, changed them, taught them to someone younger.
