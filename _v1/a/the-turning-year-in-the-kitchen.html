The Turning Year in the Kitchen

how what grows now flavours what we eat later

kept by old Jennet, who keeps the cellar

There is a jar on the shelf in the cool dark, half-full of last summer’s green tomatoes—the ones that came in at the first hard frost, when the gardener knew the year was turning. You can see them through the glass, still firm, sitting in their brine, waiting. She picked them because she had learned something with her hands: this fruit won’t ripen out there now, but it will mellow here, in time, in salt and vinegar, and in January it will taste like the last warmth of August. This is the work of the turning year—not to stop the seasons, but to carry them across the dark.

Every culture knows this. The root cellar of stone, kept cool by earth and depth. The hanging herbs above the stove—oregano and thyme browning slowly in the warmth of cooking, their flavour turning concentrated and strange, becoming new. The woman who dries apples in thin rings, the man who salts fish against the winter when nothing swims but ice. Jam made in one hour of boiling abundance, and then a long sleep in glass, waiting to be woken again on a cold morning by a spoon.

The keeping of plenty

It is not about hoarding. It is about understanding that the year is a wheel, and every spoke has its moment. When the plums are slack and heavy on the branch, the cook reaches up knowing that this particular softness must become something else—not to waste it, but to translate it. A fresh peach is a day. A dried peach is the memory of ten days, sharp and dark and worth whatever space it takes in the jar.

One season feeds another through the work of hands that will not let go.

What grows now feeds what comes latera kitchen saying

There is a kind of faith in this work—faith that winter will come, yes, but also faith that you will have the skill to meet it. The householder who pickles, who preserves, who hangs and dries and salts, is not afraid of scarcity the way a person without a cellar might be. She has held the abundance in her hands and made it into something that will last. The flavours change as the months pass. A sauerkraut in September is different from a sauerkraut in March—sharper, more alive. This too is teaching: the year does not stand still. It keeps changing even when you have stopped it in a jar. And that is the magic of the kitchen, and why people have always gathered at the table in the cold months, not just to eat, but to taste the whole year at once.

One season feeds another through the work of hands that will not let go.
