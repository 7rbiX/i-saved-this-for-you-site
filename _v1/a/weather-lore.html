a form of attention

Weather-Lore

the old sayings and signs that people still half-believe in

kept by Eleanor Thorne, who kept the almanac by the kitchen hearth

There is a smell that comes before the rain—not water itself, but something the earth gives up when the pressure shifts. You know it if you have stood in a garden long enough to stop thinking about it. The air grows thick and waiting. A farmer’s grandmother notices this without words; she simply steps inside and checks that the tea is steeped. An old sailor I knew could read it in the way his collarbone ached. These are not metaphors. They are sentences written in skin and stone and the light on leaves.

Weather-lore is the old conversation between a person and the world they live in. Not prediction in the modern sense—not the certainty of a forecast—but a dialogue of signs. Cows lying down mean rain is coming. Spiders weaving tightly mean fair weather holds. The petals of morning-glory close before a storm. These observations, gathered across generations and whispered from hand to hand, were once survival. They were the difference between a gathered harvest and hunger.

Reading the signs

What matters is not whether each saying is scientifically exact. What matters is the quality of attention it asks for. To believe the aching of bones might speak of weather coming is to stand in your own body and ask, constantly: What are you telling me? To watch the birds—do they fly high or low?—is to keep your eyes lifted. To notice when the ice first cracks on the water is to be present to the year turning. These practices are not superstition. They are a form of humility.

If the oak flowers before the ash, we’ll have a splash. If the ash flowers before the oak, we’ll have a soak.English saying

To read the world like a text is to admit the world has something to teach you if you stand still and listen.

We have lost something in gaining our glass screens and satellites. Not the facts of weather—we know those better now, in one way. But we have lost the particular knowledge that comes from belonging to a place over decades. The young ones now who grow things are returning to this, finding that the old stories hold a kind of truth that matters. Not magic. Attention. The skill of asking the land, over and over, What do you need? What are you about to do? That conversation, kept alive in saying and in watching, is weather-lore.

To read the world like a text is to admit the world has something to teach you.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The best weather-lore I ever heard came from someone who had lived in the same valley for eighty years. They could read forty kinds of cloud and knew each one by its name—not the Latin names, but the old ones passed down, the ones that meant something to the people there.
