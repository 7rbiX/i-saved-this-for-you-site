The Parlour ☞ The Hearth

a crossing remembered

The Threshold

where inside meets outside, where you step across

kept by old Ewen, the doorwright

There is a worn groove in the oak sill of the cottage, two inches deep, from the feet of people stepping through. You can feel it with your palm—one smooth place where bodies have passed a thousand times, wet shoes, bare feet, a child held against the hip. The wood around it is still hard as bone. That hollow is the threshold, and it holds something the architects never measured.

A threshold is not simply where a floor changes name. It is the place where you leave off being one thing and become another. Outside, you are exposed to weather and stranger and the wide blank sky. Inside, you are held. The old peoples knew this in their bones—they would not permit a bride to stumble crossing the threshold, or let a sick person be carried over it feet-first, or allow a gift to be handed across it without being properly received. The threshold was alive. It was watching.

A boundary that sees

Many cultures have kept a ritual at the threshold: salt, a stone, a carved guardian, a prayer spoken. The Romans had Janus, the god who looks both ways at once—into the house and out to the road. That is what a threshold does. It does not belong wholly to either world. It is the place of permission and caution both. Even now, you pause there. Your hand finds the frame.

The threshold is the last thing you command, and the first thing that commands you.a saying of builders

A threshold holds the memory of every goodbye and homecoming it has witnessed.

The strength of a good threshold is that it need not be grand. A simple dressed stone, a wooden plank worn smooth—these are enough. What matters is that it is present, that the crossing is real. In the poorest houses, it was still there. In the finest, it was honoured with carvings, with iron, with the builder’s best care. Because everyone who lives must cross it, many times, and each crossing changes something inside the person who crosses. You come home different than you left. The threshold knows it.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. In some places, a new threshold was never cut into until the house had been lived in for a year—the wood needed to know the family first.
