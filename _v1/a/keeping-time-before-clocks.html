an old practice

Keeping Time Before Clocks

how people marked the hours and the days when there was no glass and no gears

kept by Thomas, who minds the sundial

The shadow of the gnomon moves so slowly you have to stand still and watch to believe it moves at all. Hold your hand in the sun—your thumb becomes a tiny clock, the flesh between thumb and finger marking the hours, five fingers for five hours of daylight. Before the clock was boxed up in brass and wound tight with springs, people held the day this way: in the body itself, in the shadow on stone, in the ache of light across the back of the hand.

The sundial is patient. It asks you to look up, to notice where the sun hangs. A shadow on a stone slab, or scratched into clay, or drawn in the dust of a courtyard—it works without winding, without a spring ever failing. The waterman in ancient Egypt knew something simpler: he kept a tall jar with marks scratched down its inside, and filled it from the river, watching the waterline descend as the night wore on. Hour after hour, marked by the falling of water.

The Grain of the Day

When you live by the sun and the water and the length of your own shadow, time is not a line you walk along. It is a thing that breathes. It comes in seasons, in the weather-lore passed from mouth to mouth—when the swallows turn, when the frost comes, when the light grows thin enough to see through. You know when to plant because your people have watched the year turn a hundred times. You know when to gather the harvest by touch, by the weight of grain in your palm, not by a number on a dial.

Time without a clock is time without hurry.

There is something lost when you trade the shadow for the tick. You stop looking up. You stop asking the sun where you are. But there is something gained too—you can meet at the same moment across a distance, you can promise to meet a friend in Rome and a friend in Cairo at precisely the same breath of the day. The clock makes the world smaller and more legible. It makes the world run at the same speed everywhere at once.

Yet in the old way there was a kind of freedom. The hours bent to the season. In winter they grew shorter, in summer they stretched long—the day itself changed shape. You were not a prisoner of even division. You were a watcher. You were time-less in the way that matters: not without time, but not slave to it. The shadow fell where it fell. The water dropped grain by grain. And you, standing in the sun with your hand held up against the brightness, you knew where you were in the day because you had looked.

Time without a clock is time without hurry.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The finger-hour system survives in some old books—a way of teaching children the sun’s journey by counting on the hand itself, the breadth of a finger marking about fifteen minutes of arc across the sky.
