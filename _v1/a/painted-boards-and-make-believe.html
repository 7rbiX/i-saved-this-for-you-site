A CRAFT REMEMBERED

Painted Boards and Make-Believe

how a stage is built from air and intention

kept by Thom Whitley, who painted scenery for the old circuit

A painted cloth, damp with sizing, hangs from a rail. Two men lift it into place. The canvas trembles — pale blue-grey, with a few dark brushstrokes that mean ‘distant trees’ — and the barn becomes a forest. It is nothing. It is everything. Someone in the audience leans forward. A child grips her mother’s hand. The pretence has begun, not because anyone was fooled, but because everyone agreed, all at once, that they would be.

The stage is the smallest thing that needs to happen. A line drawn on the ground. A platform six boards high. A bare wall with light falling across it. The scene painter’s job is not to create a palace, exactly — it is to create the permission to see one. A few strokes of shadow where a window should be. A wash of gold at the top for ‘throne-light.’ The mind does the rest. Your mind does it. Mine does it. The child certainly does.

The Compact

There is a silent contract between the stage and the dark. The people on the boards move inside a frame of light, and the people in the dark agree to follow the rules of this place — this room that is also a wood, a city, a dream. No one checks for an actual throne. No one counts the trees. What matters is the gesture: the actor’s hand reaching toward the backdrop as if it were distance itself. The audience’s breath, held.

Four boards and a belief make a theatre.a strollers’ saying

The stage is a door, and paint is the key.

A woman paints scenery in an attic. She has never been to Venice, but she paints blue water and gondolas, remembering what she read in a story her own mother read aloud. Fifty people will sit in that dark. Some will have travelled to cities; some will never leave this town. For two hours, they will all float on the same painted canal. They will weep. They will laugh. They will believe. And afterward, when the cloth comes down and the boards are swept, they will carry the wood-smoke and the belief home with them, and tell their families: I was in Venice once.

The stage is a door, and paint is the key.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The simplest stages are often the truest — a lantern, a voice, and the space where the audience’s hope lives.
