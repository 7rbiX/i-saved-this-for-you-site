The Moon’s Many Faces

why one object has held so many names and stories

kept by Thomas, who kept the night watch

The moon does what no other star can do: she changes her face. Night after night, you can watch her wax from a sliver to fullness, feel her pull at the dark water and your own blood. A shepherd in Mesopotamia saw her first as a crescent and called her the horn of the bull. A Maori navigator saw her as a guide and knew her by her phases like a language. A mother saw her rise over her sleeping children and called her by yet another name — protector, keeper, thief of sleep. The same object, seen from villages and deserts and ships and small rooms, became a thousand names.

This is why the moon has been sacred. Not because she is distant or strange — she is close enough to hold, near enough to feel like company. She is the one celestial thing that lives in time with us, that you can actually watch change from one night to the next. Stars are fixed. The sun is obvious. But the moon is a mirror turned toward us, showing us something different each time we look.

The Measure of Days

Before any clock, the moon kept time. A woman knew her own body by the moon’s face. Plantings happened in the bright half, harvestings in the dark. A month — the word itself is the moon’s — was a real thing you could see, a circle of knowing. Entire calendars grew from watching her. The Hebrew calendar, the Islamic calendar, the lunisolar calendars of Asia and the Pacific: they all trusted the moon as the true keeper of when. And because time is the deepest hunger of human life, the moon became sacred.

The moon is a friend for the lonesome to talk to.a ballad saying

She is the one celestial thing that changes visibly, that you can actually watch becoming, as if she were alive.

In every culture, she has been both witness and trickster, both stable and wild. She makes the tides obey her. She looks back at you with the same face for a thousand years, and yet she is never quite the same. This close companion has been called Selene, Luna, Hecate, Mama Quilla, Tsukiyomi, Coyolxauhqui. She has been virgin and mother and crone. She has been loved and feared and named in bitter songs. And we keep on looking up at her because she alone among the lights of the sky admits what we know: that all things change, that being alive means becoming something different each night, and that this too is beautiful.

She is the one celestial thing that changes visibly, that you can actually watch becoming, as if she were alive.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The moon has no light of her own — she borrows it from the sun and gives it back to us. There is something true in that, about what it means to be a mirror, to receive and return what is given.
