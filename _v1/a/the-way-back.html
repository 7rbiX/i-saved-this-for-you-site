The Way Back

on returning home after years away

kept by Eliza Hartwell, keeper of the morning post

The kitchen light is still that yellow. Not a metaphor—that exact colour, the one you saw at six in the morning when you were ten, stealing toast before anyone woke. You stand in the doorway and it hits you in the chest like an old song you had forgotten you knew.

Coming home is not the thing they tell you it will be. It is not the embrace at the door, though that happens too. It is this: the shock of recognising your own place and not quite knowing it. The street corner where you learned to ride a bicycle is both smaller and larger than it was. The route your feet know by heart wants to take you somewhere you no longer need to go. You unlock the front door and half-expect a stranger to answer.

The strangeness of return

A place does not wait for you. That is the first hard thing to understand. While you have been away—learning the streets of another city, the weight of different weather, the particular loneliness of being foreign—this place has gone on living without you. It has changed in ways you did not authorise. Your bedroom is smaller. The tree in the front garden is taller. People you knew have grown middle-aged. The small shop on the corner where you bought sweets is now a phone repair place, and you grieve for it as though it were a person.

Home is the place where, when you have to go there, they have to take you in.Robert Frost

But homecoming is not simple shelter. It is a negotiation between the person you were and the person you have become. You move through these rooms like a translator, finding the words that fit both languages. You sit at the kitchen table where you have sat a thousand times and feel yourself flickering between two selves—the child who lived here and the stranger who has just arrived.

A place you thought you knew becomes half-foreign, and that is how you know you have truly left.

The real homecoming is quieter than arrival. It happens when you stop looking for what has changed and start noticing what has held. The way your mother still folds the towels. The crack in the stone step that you remember stepping over. The ghost of yourself in the hallway, small and certain, moving through a world that felt infinite. You begin to understand that home was never just a place. It was a knowing—and you carried it with you all along, even when you could not name it. Now you are learning to hold both: the place you had to leave and the person you had to become.

A place you thought you knew becomes half-foreign, and that is how you know you have truly left.
