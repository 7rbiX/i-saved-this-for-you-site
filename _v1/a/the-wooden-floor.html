The Wooden Floor

what a dance floor remembers

kept by Thomas, keeper of old halls

The wood is pale gold in certain light, darker where a thousand shoes have worn away the finish, leaving shadows like a map of all the bodies that have stood there. You can feel it the moment your foot touches down—that particular springiness, the slight give that is not softness but something deeper, as if the floor has learned to catch you. It holds the warmth of the room, the sweat, the weight. When the music stops and you stand still, the boards are still singing a little, still holding the hum of what just happened.

A dance floor is a conversation between the body and the earth, held at shoulder height. It knows every stumble and every soaring turn. There are scuffs from the pivot of a thousand waltzes, small gouges where heels have landed too hard, the faint smell of rosin rising when the weight shifts. The boards are laid with grain running the same direction, which is not accident—it makes the spring even, it helps the body know where it is.

The memory held in grain

Wood remembers. You can see it in the fine scratches that fan out from the edge of the stage, or from where chairs were pushed back a thousand nights ago. The color is never even—the edges are lighter where the air has faded them, the centre deeper from the oils of moving feet. And under it all, holding it together, the wood breathes with the seasons, swelling and contracting, never still, always answering the room.

The body knows the way homea saying

There is something about the wood that makes the body remember itself. Not just the muscle, but something older—the knowledge that we are animals that move, that dance, that gather. The floor does not judge how you move across it. It does not care if you are perfect. It only asks that you give your weight to it, that you trust the spring, that you meet it in the particular way your body meets any room it enters.

A dance floor is the only place where a thousand different stories are written in the same language.

After the dancing is finished, after the last couple has left and the chairs are stacked, the floor sits in the dark and holds what it has been given. In the morning, the first light will catch the dust rising from it—all those feet still moving through the air. The wood will be ready again, patient, waiting. That is what a floor is for: to hold us as we learn to fly.

A dance floor is the only place where a thousand different stories are written in the same language.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The best dance floors have a story in their wood—sometimes you can feel it in the particular way the spring catches your heel.
