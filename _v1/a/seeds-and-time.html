a human truth

Seeds and Time

the patience of waiting, the gamble of faith

kept by Thomas, who keeps the seed box

You push a bean into soil and cover it with your thumb. It sits. For weeks it sits. No sign that anything you did mattered. Your finger could have drawn a line in mud; the bean could have been a stone. This is the first lesson a garden teaches, and it arrives too late for most of us—we are creatures of haste, and seeds are creatures of deliberation.

In the old seed catalogues, they printed what they called the “days to maturity”: lettuce in 60 days, carrots in 80, tomatoes in 85. Numbers that sound like promises. But they never printed the other count—the days you will stare at bare soil wondering if you made a mistake. The days you will water the same patch and see only mud, only patience demanded, only nothing.

The bargain we strike

When you plant a seed, you enter into a strange contract. You do your part: the soil, the water, the light, the small ceremony of care. And then you wait for the earth to hold up its end. But the earth does not negotiate. It does not hurry. It does not even guarantee. A seed may rot. A frost may come. A bird may dig it up.

In every concealment there is also a revelationa saying

We plant seeds to practise faith in something we cannot control.

Yet people have done this—this exact thing, this act of hope disguised as common sense—for ten thousand years. A woman in a village whose name is lost planted a seed. She had no certainty. She had hunger, and spring, and the knowledge that something had grown before. She did her work and trusted. This is not heroic. This is simply what it means to be human and alive on the earth.

The day the first green breaks the crust of soil is a small miracle, and you will never quite stop feeling it as one, no matter how many times you have planted. You did this. You and the earth, together. You learned, in those weeks of nothing, that you are not alone in your wanting. The seed was always tending toward the light. You were both patient. You were both faithful. And the soil kept its promise.

We plant seeds to practise faith in something we cannot control.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. Keep your oldest seeds in a cool, dark corner—they are still dreaming.
