The Parlour ☞ The Map Room

a geography of memory

The Country of Childhood

the unmappable territory we all carry

kept by Martin, who keeps the old atlases

There is a room with green walls and a shelf that holds your small weight. Morning light comes in at a particular angle—you remember the dust-motes spinning in it, remember the sound of your own breathing when you were alone there. You could draw it now: the window-latch, the crack in the plaster shaped like a running hare, the corner where you kept a small wooden box. And yet if you walked into that room tomorrow, it would not be there. Not as you carry it.

The country of childhood is a place everyone has lived, and no two people have lived in the same one. It exists in perfect detail behind your ribs—every sound, every smell, the exact quality of afternoon light on a particular day in October when you were seven, or nine, or seventeen. You have not lost it. You carry it.

A geography only you can read

Childhood has its own cartography. It is measured not in miles but in the time it took to walk across a field, or the number of times you climbed the same apple tree. A village-girl knows every hollow where water collects; a city child knows the safe corners and the dangerous ones, the route home with your eyes shut. The geography is perfect and strange—a fence can loom larger than mountains; a garden can contain the whole world. And all of it, every sacred inch, remains exact in your hands.

Here is the mystery: this place is more real than the one where you stand now. You remember the weight of the door, the smell of it, the way the hinge sang. You could recognise the sound of a particular voice calling your name—your own name, the version of you only that voice ever knew. Yet you cannot return there. Not as yourself, and not because the place has changed. Because you are looking at it now with different eyes, and you have learned what all of us learn: that we live in two countries at once.

The days that are still inside you are more important than the days that have passed.a saying, often true

You carry it in your hands. You have never left.

This is why grown people sometimes stop in a doorway and stand very still. Why a particular sound—a door-close, a child’s laugh, an old song—can overturn you. Why we keep photographs, why we return to the places of our childhood wanting the country to be there, unchanged. The place on the map is just a place. But the one inside you is a country entire. You are its ambassador, its memory-keeper, its border-guard. You were never meant to draw it for anyone else. You were only meant to know it, completely, the way your hands know it. The way you know it still.

You carry it in your hands. You have never left.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. A cartographer of the outer world can show you where these places are. But the inner map is yours alone to hold.
