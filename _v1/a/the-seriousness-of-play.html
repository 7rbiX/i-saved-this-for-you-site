a truth about children

The Seriousness of Play

why a game is not small

kept by Beatrice, who watches from the garden wall

Watch a child’s face when a rule is broken. Not the face of someone who shrugs at disorder—but the face of a lawmaker whose constitution has been torn. There is outrage in it, yes, but also something graver: the shock of injustice. A child at play is not passing time. A child at play is building the world.

This is what adults forget, standing outside the schoolyard fence. We see tag and think it idle—just running, just noise. But a child in that game is not running for the sake of running. She is negotiating the laws of chase. She is learning what fairness feels like when it lives in her own body. The one who is ‘it’ knows the weight of that word. Base is not just a chalk mark; it is sanctuary, asylum, the place where mercy lives. A child learns mercy by standing in base.

The grammar of becoming

Every game a child invents is a small government. Who goes first? That question settles questions of worth. How many times can you hide if you are counting? That’s about trust, about whether the world keeps its promises. What happens if you cheat? That’s about what you can live with when you look in the mirror. These are not small matters wrapped in play—they are the deepest questions, dressed in the language a child can speak.

Play is the work of childhood.a saying among those who tend to children

A child at play is not passing time—she is building the grammar of how to be human.

And the territories! The sprawl of a game—the oak tree is the castle, the gutter is the moat, the gap between the fence slats is the prison. A child will defend these boundaries with a fierceness that looks out of proportion until you remember: she is defending her own invention. She is saying to the world, this thing I made—it matters. I know the rules, and you will obey them because we agreed. In that moment, she is learning that reality is not something that happens to her. It is something she can make.

When the game ends and she comes in for supper, flushed and breathing hard, don’t ask her what she was doing. She will struggle to explain, because it was not a ‘what’—it was a ‘becoming.’ She was becoming the kind of person who keeps her word, who knows when to bend a rule and when to hold it sacred, who can be both a maker and a keeper of what others have made. That is not small work. That is not small joy.

A child at play is building the grammar of how to be human.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. Some afternoons, a child will play alone, and that is its own thing—the invention of whole worlds, the conjuring of companions who will do exactly as she wishes. Perhaps that teaches her something too about power, and loneliness, and the difference.
