The Parlour ☞ The Toy Box

a gift of cloth and clay

Dolls Across the World

the doll that belongs to every child and none

kept by Agnes, who minds the rag-bag

A cloth face stitched in two dozen places, button eyes, a mouth of red thread—here is the doll a child has held so long the seams have split and closed again from use. It is not pretty anymore. It is kin. In a mountain village in Peru, a grandmother winds alpaca wool around a wooden rod, adds a face of embroidered yarn, and hands it to her grandson. In a Japanese home, a doll carved from cherry wood, smooth from fingers, wears a kimono sewn from scraps of real silk. In a village in Ghana, a girl wraps cloth around a water gourd, paints eyes with ash, and has made something that knows her voice. The doll has no inventor. It is as old as the need to say: here, I have made you someone to hold while I hold something else.

What enters into a doll when a child gives it a name? What lives in the thing? An archaeologist finds clay dolls in the ruins of Çatalhöyük, eight thousand years old, with the marks of small thumbs still pressed into the clay. They do not prove anything but presence—here, a child was, and here is what the child’s hands made small enough to carry. To hold a doll is to hold power: to command a life, to teach it, to forgive it, to make it better than the world. A child dresses her doll for the dance the doll was too shy to attend. A child heals the doll’s sorrows with a kiss. The doll asks nothing but belief.

The Doll as Teacher

In every village, the doll learns what a child needs to practice: how to hold a baby, how to speak kindness, how to keep the small and breakable safe. A Navajo mother makes a figure of cornhusk and sits her daughter beside her to watch. A Nigerian child braids the doll’s hair as she has seen her mother braid. The doll becomes a citizen of its child’s world—it eats what the child eats, believes what the child believes, goes where the child goes. It is the first intimate companion, the first in a long line of selves a person will learn to be.

A doll is what a child gives away, knowing it will come home.

We are never deceived by the doll; we only ever deceive ourselves with the doll—and that is the whole of play.a saying

A doll is worn down by love. The paint fades where fingers found the doll’s face. The fabric tears at the joints—the places where the doll bent and bent until it learned to move like something alive. Some dolls are wrapped in cloth and given to younger children, who add their own thumbprints to the old marks. Some are buried or burned in ceremonies, released from their service. Some sit in a corner of memory, and a grown person, seeing them, is startled to feel the weight of the small hand that held them. The doll taught the child its first truth: that we are moved by what we make, that the powerless become powerful through the gift we give them, that love is not small because its vessel is small.

A doll is what a child gives away, knowing it will come home.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. In some traditions, a doll is given a name in a ceremony so it may receive a child’s prayers and keep them safe until they are grown.
  2. The oldest known dolls are made of clay, carved bone, and wood—materials that last because children loved them enough to keep them.
