The Lock of Hair

when a curl of the beloved became a relic

kept by Harriet, who keeps the old drawers

A curl, fine as silk thread, wound on paper yellowed now to the color of old ivory. The ribbon tied around it is pale blue, or was—time has made it nearly grey. There is no name on the paper, no date. Just the weight of it in your palm, lighter than nothing, and the knowledge that this is all that remains of someone. A piece of skin that grew, that was brushed and braided, that lay against a shoulder. A piece that left the body and never had to leave the love.

People have done this forever. When the beloved died—child, wife, soldier, friend—a mother would take scissors, with her hands shaking or steady, and cut a small memory from the head of the dead. Sometimes braided, sometimes in an envelope marked with the date of dying. Sometimes kept in a locket that lay against the heart. A thing so small you could hold it in a closed fist, but it meant this person was not entirely gone. Some piece of their flesh, still here. Still warm, almost, with the idea of them.

The strange arithmetic of grief

We keep our dead in portraits, in words, in stories told too many times. We keep them in objects they held—a watch, a cup, a pen. But the lock of hair was different. It was them. Not a thing they owned or made, but a thing they grew. It had lived on their skin. It had been part of the body that moved and spoke and loved and suffered. To hold it was to hold the thinnest edge of the actual person, a thread between the living world and the gone.

Absence becomes presence when you can hold it in your hand.

In the hair of those we have lost, we touch the boundary between presence and memory, between what was and what remains.a saying

Now we photograph everything. The dead live in color on our screens. But there is something the photograph cannot do—it cannot be touched, held, worn. A lock of hair knows your fingers. It knows the particular warmth of the hand that misses. And because it was once alive, was once part of a living person, it holds a kind of company that an image cannot match. You can sleep with it under your pillow. You can take it on a journey. It will not fade or develop spots or go out of focus. It is a covenant with the dead, the smallest one, the most lasting: I will carry this. I will remember that you existed. Your body is gone, but this thread—this thread I keep.

Absence becomes presence when you can hold it in your hand.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. It’s believed the Victorians, especially, kept locks of hair as mourning jewelry—coiled into brooches, bracelets, even hair wreaths. The craft was almost an art. But the habit is far older and far wider, found in cultures across centuries and continents. A simple human hunger to hold what has gone.
