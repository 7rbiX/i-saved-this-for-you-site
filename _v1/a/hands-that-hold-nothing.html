a craft remembered

Hands That Hold Nothing

the strange life of puppets and marionettes

kept by Thomas Bridewell, who mended the strings

A woman in a threadbare velvet coat stands in the wings with her fingers already moving. The puppet has not yet lifted from the table, but her hands are dancing — two fingers in the head-knot, one in each wrist. She is not looking at the thing she holds. She is looking at the audience in the dark, and her hands know where the ground is, where the light is, where the heart lives. The puppet does not move until the moving stops. Then it is alive.

There is a strangeness to puppetry that most people never quite name. It is not the magic of acting — that is one person becoming another. A puppet is the opposite. It is nothing becoming something. A scrap of cloth, a wooden joint, a nub of head stuffed with horsehair — these things weigh almost nothing. The strings weigh nothing. But the minute the right hands find the knots, the minute the joints move the way cloth and wood are hinged to move, something walks into the room that did not exist a moment before. It is not pretend. It is not a trick. It is summoning.

The weight of the invisible

The craft lives in knowing exactly where the pivot is — the shoulder-joint, the elbow, the place where felt gives way to something that bends. A puppeteer’s hand must feel the weight of what it is not holding. There is a technique that lives in the wrist, in the small bones of the fingers. Too much force and the figure jerks like something broken. Too little and it droops, dead. The strings themselves teach: they sing under tension. A good puppeteer learns to play them like an instrument.

I do not pull the strings; the strings pull me where we need to go.a saying among the players

A puppet has no will except the will it is given, and yet somehow it becomes a separate thing.

This is why the best puppets are always slightly worn. A joint that moves easily has been moved ten thousand times. A painted eye has been looked through by hands in the dark for decades, for centuries maybe. The wood remembers. The cloth remembers. There is a dignity in the used thing, the thing so deeply handled that it has become real in the way only objects become real — through touch, through intention, through the repeated small acts of making it matter. You can love a puppet. People do. They grieve it when the string snaps for the last time.

And perhaps that is the oldest magic of all: not that we can make something move, but that in moving it, we make something worth loving. The hands vanish. The strings become invisible. What remains is a presence in the circle of light — a sovereign, a beggar, a broken heart — as real as grief, as true as the person who reached into nothing and made it live.

A puppet has no will except the will it is given, and yet somehow it becomes a separate thing.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The best-kept marionettes in Europe are worn smooth by hand, the joints dark with the oils of centuries of fingers. You can run your thumb into the groove of a shoulder and feel every generation of the puppeteer’s touch.
