a bridge remembered

The Bridge That Tea Made

one small true story of the cup crossing oceans and walls

kept by Eleanor, who keeps the old letters

There is a packet of letters in a London archive, water-stained and small. They were written by an English merchant’s wife in Canton in 1838, to her sister at home. In the margins and between the lines, she describes something no one had taught her to expect: friendship with her Chinese neighbours. Not the formal kind, but real. They began drinking tea together.

The woman’s name was Susan. She knew nothing about tea when she arrived—for her it was what you drank at home, bitter and milked, from whatever the grocer sold. Here in Canton, an older woman whose name Susan learned to write phonetically, with a brush, sat with her at a table and showed her the difference between leaves that matter and leaves that don’t. The silence between them was not awkward. It was a language.

A small breach in the wall

This happened during a time when the world was breaking open—the trade wars, the gunboats, the empire at its most certain and cruel. Tea was the reason for all of it: the British wanted it, China had it, and the world was burning. And yet here were two women on opposite sides of that fire, learning to drink together. It was not large enough to change history. But it was large enough to change two lives.

She has shown me that the cup is not merely a thing one drinks from, but a teacher, and a door.from Susan’s letter, 1839

The cup is a thing so small that empires forget it holds the power to make us human to each other.

Susan’s sister never met the woman in Canton. She only had the letters, full of wonder and small details about the flowers on the cups, the smell of the leaves, the way a particular tea tastes different depending on the water, the season, the hand that pours it. Susan lived there six years. When she left, she left behind a friendship—one that the trade routes would soon make impossible. But the letters remained. In them, the bridge stays standing.

The cup is a thing so small that empires forget it holds the power to make us human to each other.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. Susan Marsden’s letters are held in the East India Company archive (British Library reference IOR/H/753). A small monument to what was possible, even then.
