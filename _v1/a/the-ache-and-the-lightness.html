what the body keeps

The Ache and the Lightness

the price and the gift of moving well

kept by Harriet, who remembers the spring

The next morning, you reach for a coffee cup and your shoulder protests. Not pain, quite—something friendlier than that. A tenderness. A knowing. The muscles remember what they did last night, and they tell you about it as you move through your kitchen, slower than usual, lighter somehow, as if you have been remade a little.

There is a peculiar grace in the ache of dancing. It is not the ache of injury or of work done grudgingly. It is the ache of joy expressed through the body—the proof that you asked everything of yourself and the self answered. Your calves remember the push-off into the air. Your lower back knows the twist and the give. Even your feet, which you rarely think about, speak up when you put them on the ground, reminding you how far they travelled.

The body’s account

What is strange is how the ache coexists with a lightness that seems to defy it. You move through the day differently. Your breathing is easier. Your spine holds you taller without trying. The tension you carry in your neck—the one that comes from looking at screens and looking down at worry—has loosened. Dance does this. It reminds the body that it is meant to move in large, generous ways, and the body believes it for a while, even after the music stops.

The legs remember what the mind forgets.a saying

You are lighter because you have moved, and you ache because the moving mattered.

There is also a change that doesn’t show up as soreness. A clarity. A gentleness toward yourself. After you have danced—really danced, given yourself to it—something in you loosens that was held tight. Perhaps it is because for those hours you stopped thinking about yourself as a problem to be solved, stopped judging the shape you make or the noise you make. You simply moved, and the body, when it is allowed to do what it was made to do, teaches you something true about being alive. That lesson sits in your muscles for days.

The ache fades. By Thursday it is mostly gone. But the other thing—that lightness, that forgetting-how-to-worry, that sense of being on good terms with your own skin—that lingers longer. It is a gift wrapped in soreness, a reminder that to be fully alive sometimes means to be sore, and to be sore in this way is to be blessed.

You are lighter because you have moved, and you ache because the moving mattered.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. The particular ache of different dances lives in different places. Jive dancers know their knees. Ballet dancers, their feet and hips. Folk dancers, the deep inside of the thigh. Each dance leaves its own map on the body.
