When It Breaks, It Speaks

the language of repair and what we learn from listening

kept by Harriet Moss, who tends the old things

The clock was silent for forty years before it reached your bench. Not the silence of sleep—the silence of arrest. You listen first, before your hands touch anything. You hold it to your ear and hear not ticking but weight: the heaviness of gears that have forgotten how to turn. The case smells like the closed drawer it came from, dust and the ghosts of oils gone thick. You are not angry with it. You are curious the way you might be curious about a letter that arrived sealed.

The hands tell you something already. One is longer, thinner—the minute hand. It points to seven, and the hour hand rests between three and four. The clock stopped in an afternoon sometime in the 1980s, in the middle of a Tuesday no one remembers. You note the position. You will return to this moment.

What the dial does not say

You open the case. The mechanism is smaller than people expect—everything is always smaller than the sound it makes. Three jewels catch the light where they should. The balance wheel is still. The escapement, that little gate that lets time out one tick at a time, is clogged with varnish the colour of old honey. This is the culprit and also the story: someone loved this clock enough to try to preserve it, sealing it away, and in sealing it away, sealed in its death.

Things break not because they are old but because they are loveda saying

You dissolve the old varnish in solvent so gentle it might be prayer. You do not rush. A gear emerges like a thing remembering its own shape. You oil it—true horological oil, the exact viscosity, the colour of pale wine. The pivot spins in your palm, smooth as breath. You wind the mainspring with the key that came in the case, and you feel the coil gather its strength, as if you are filling a lung with air.

The first tick is not a sound—it is a small miracle, the clock choosing to live again

It runs for five minutes, and you let it. You set the hands to the correct time—not to now, but to when it stopped, so you can say to whoever brings it here: your clock was here, in this moment, and now it is here again, whole. The ticking fills the silence it left behind. You wrap it in cotton and set it in a box with care. Somewhere, someone will open this box and hear the clock they thought was gone. They will cry, perhaps, or simply listen, the way you are listening now to the language of all things that break and are brought back to speak.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. I have never mended a watch myself, but I have watched a mender work, and the silence in the room was louder than the ticking.
  2. If you are bringing an old clock to someone with gentle hands, do not apologize. It has been waiting.
