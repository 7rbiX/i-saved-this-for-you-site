a way of being known

Handwriting

the body’s mark on the page

kept by M. Hayworth, who has kept the school ledgers

There is a slant to it, a pressure, a particular way the ink catches where the nib hesitates. I have sat in the corner of the schoolroom for twenty years watching children learn to hold the pen, and I know this: no two hands are the same. Not one. A capital T made by Thomas looks nothing like a T made by his sister Violet, though she has watched him form it a thousand times. His comes down like a dropped weight. Hers floats, careful. These small differences are not mistakes. They are proof.

We call it penmanship, as if there is one right way, a standard taught from the copybook. But the body will not be standardized. It arrives at the page with its own habits—the tremor of an old hand, the impatience of a fast mind, the caution of someone learning to trust the pen. A child born lefthanded will find her own anchor point, her own angle, no matter what the teacher insists. The hand knows what it knows.

The fingerprint of the soul

A letter comes unsigned and you know who wrote it. Not from the words alone, though those matter—but from the shape of the w, the height of the d, the way a particular person dots an i (high and firm, or soft and distant). Police and forgerers have always known this: a hand cannot lie. It is older than language. It is bone and muscle, habit and hurt, time spent holding a pen.

Every stroke of the pen is a small act of becominga saying

When you type, the machine stands between you and the word. The letters come out identical, impersonal. But handwriting is direct—fingers to nib to paper—and the page receives not just the content but the sender. The nervousness of a proposal written in pencil. The care in a child’s careful letters spelling out a name. The fury in the violent underlining of something that matters. The hand is always confessing.

A handwritten word holds the hand that made it.

This is why we keep old letters. Not for the information alone, but to hold the page that was held. To see the pressure where the pen nearly broke the paper in saying something urgent. To brush our thumb across the ink that has not moved in eighty years. We are reading not the words but the person. The particular, unrepeatable human being who picked up the pen on a winter morning and wrote for you. That is a thing a machine cannot give. It is only ever human, only ever now, only ever this hand reaching toward another.

Children who are taught only to type will lose something their grandmothers knew by muscle—that the way you move through the world is written in the way you write.

A handwritten word holds the hand that made it.

Notes, & afterthoughts
  1. There is a reason we save letters in boxes, pressed between quilts, returned to again and again. We are holding the hand.
