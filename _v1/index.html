The Commonplace Book

Homo sum, humani nihil a me alienum puto — I am human, and nothing human is foreign to me.

An almanac of culture, art & the things worth keeping · kept by many hands

The Parlour☞The Rooms☞The Keepers☞The Guest-Book☞Colophon

Welcome, traveller. Wipe your feet, hang your coat on the hook, and come in out of the weather — the kettle is on.

This is a commonplace book. That is an old word, and a good one. It is the name for a plain notebook into which a person once copied, by hand, the things they could not bear to lose — a recipe, a remedy, a line of a song, the way to graft an apple, the shape of a child’s hand drawn round with a pencil. A commonplace book belonged to no one important and to everyone who loved it. It was where a life kept the things that held it together.

Ours is kept the same way, by a great many hands, about the small and durable arts of being human — the made thing, the sung thing, the cooked thing, the danced thing, the believed thing. There is nothing here that will make you faster or richer. There are quilts and lullabies and the smell of bread, the names people gave the stars, the dignity of a mended chair. We do not think these are trifles. We think they are the load-bearing walls of a life.

Everything you will read was written by people sitting up late, trying to say a true thing kindly. Every picture is a real object, made by a real pair of hands, most of them long gone and most of them never famous — which is, when you think about it, the truest signature in the whole history of art. Wander where you like. Every room is a door we left open on purpose. And before you go back out into the weather, sign the guest-book, so the next traveller knows you passed this way.
